This case demonstrates that GDPR enforcement can be triggered by persistent individual complaints, and that forced consent—making consent a condition of service—is unlawful. It sets a precedent for similar loyalty programs across Europe. The fine was imposed five years after the privacy advocate first complained. The DPA found four separate infringements, with the central issue being that consent was not freely given, specific, or properly informed.

**Background**: GDPR requires that consent for data processing must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous. Forced consent, where a service is conditional on agreeing to marketing, violates these requirements. The maximum fine under GDPR is €20 million or 4% of global revenue.

## [Dark Matter Search Nears Breakthrough with Liquid Xenon Detectors](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/06/18/1138755/search-for-dark-matter-blown-wide-open/) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Multiple underground liquid xenon detectors, including XENONnT, LUX, and PandaX, are advancing toward the first direct detection of dark matter, potentially within the next few years. Directly detecting dark matter would confirm its particle nature and revolutionize our understanding of the universe's composition, as dark matter constitutes about 85% of all matter. These detectors use dual-phase time projection chambers filled with liquid xenon to detect rare nuclear recoils from dark matter interactions, while being shielded deep underground to reduce background noise.

rss · MIT Technology Review · Jun 18, 09:00

**Background**: Dark matter is an invisible substance inferred from gravitational effects on galaxies and cosmic structures, but it has never been directly detected. Direct detection experiments aim to observe the faint recoil of a dark matter particle hitting an atomic nucleus in a sensitive detector. Liquid xenon is a popular target material because it produces both scintillation light and ionization signals, allowing precise event reconstruction.
